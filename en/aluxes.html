The Aluxes, known in Mayan as alux in singular and aluxo'ob in plural, they're small goblins or elves that have inhabited the Mayan cosmogony since ancient times. These criatures, which resemble humans but with a height approximately to the knees, are described as guardians of the nature, the sowing and the Mayan communities. Their home remains in hidden places like jungles, caves and cenotes, where they act as guardians and protagonists of numerous legends.

In the Mayan communities, many people claim to have seen them or have witnessed their shenanigans. Among their attributed pranks are moving objects, causing travelers to get lost in the jungles or making noises to scare. However, they're also considered kind beings, especially with those who work in the fields, such as farmers and peasants, or with those who leave offerings in their territory. These offerings, along with the construction of altars, are considered essential when starting a home or a sowing, as it's believed that the aluxes help to protect the fields, summoning the rain and watching over during the night.

The locals that work in the field assure that they can perceive them through the wind, describing that they usually whistle, throw pebbles or drop objects as signs of their presence. Additionally, they act like guardians against thieves or people who could cause damage to the environment. It's said that these goblins dress like ancient Mayans and, although they can protect the travelers and locals that respect their space, they also unleash their anger if someone invokes them imprudently or doesn't ask permission to access sacred places like forests or cenotes.

The belief on the aluxes remains alive today. For many, they represent a connection with the ancient Mayan culture and a reminder of the importance of respecting the nature and their invisible guardians.
